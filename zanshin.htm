"Zanshin" is a martial arts term that loosely translates to "remaining mind". According to Louis Frederic in "A Dictionary of the Martial Arts", it is "a state of vigilance and alertness of the contest, before and after striking the opponent, so that he or she may be ready for any further offensive."


In his book, "The Essence of Okinawan Karate-do", Shoshin Nagamine says this about zanshin: [it] is the state of mind where one is able to proceed from the end of one movement to the next movement freely. It literally means being absolutely attentive to the next move right after the previous move. In zanshin the mind focuses completely on the body's movement. .... Being at one with the movement so that no outside force can interrupt this oneness is the state of zanshin.


Gichin Funakoshi, the father of modern karate, in "Karate-do Kyohan" says: [competitors] must not shift their eyes or move their limbs needlessly. This is called keeping the "mind in reserve," that is; preserving one's alertness (literally, "remaining mind," or zanshin.


In many sports athletes will talk about being in the "zone". This is a form of zanshin. In football a running back is able to dodge and weave his way through the defenders. He senses openings almost before they are there. He knows where the defenders are now and where they're going to be next. This total awareness is Zanshin.
